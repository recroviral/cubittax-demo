Frequently Asked Questions

  • What are the key Canadian tax deadlines?

    In Canada, some important tax deadlines include the individual income tax return filing deadline, which is April 30th for most individuals. If you or your spouse or common-law partner are self-employed, the deadline is June 15th, but any taxes owed are still due on April 30th. The corporate tax return filing deadline for businesses is typically six months after the fiscal year-end.

  • What is the difference between RRSP and TFSA contributions?

    Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP) contributions are tax-deductible, and the investment income grows tax-deferred until withdrawal, typically during retirement. Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) contributions are not tax-deductible, but any investment income or capital gains earned within a TFSA are tax-free upon withdrawal. TFSAs are more flexible for short-term savings goals, while RRSPs are designed for long-term retirement savings.

  • What tax credits are available for families in Canada?

    Canada offers several tax credits and benefits for families, including the Canada Child Benefit (CCB), the Child Disability Benefit (CDB), and the Child Fitness Tax Credit. These programs provide financial assistance to families with children, especially those with lower incomes or children with disabilities. Additionally, there are tax credits like the Child Care Expense Deduction and the Adoption Expense Tax Credit.

  • What is the Goods and Services Tax (GST) in Canada?

    The Goods and Services Tax (GST) is a federal value-added tax in Canada. It is a 5% tax applied to most goods and services sold in Canada. Some provinces have harmonized their provincial sales tax with the GST, creating a Harmonized Sales Tax (HST). The HST rates vary by province. GST/HST registration is required for businesses with annual revenues exceeding a certain threshold.

  • What are the tax implications of selling a property in Canada?

    When you sell a property in Canada, you may be subject to capital gains tax on any profit earned from the sale. However, the sale of your primary residence is usually exempt from capital gains tax. For other properties, 50% of the capital gain is taxable. Proper record-keeping and understanding the principal residence rules can help minimize your tax liability when selling property in Canada.
